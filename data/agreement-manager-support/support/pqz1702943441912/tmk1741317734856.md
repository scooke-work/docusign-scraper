# Best Practice: Writing Definitions for AI Detection

Read about tips for writing good definitions for the AI detection setup process. These
definitions are an important part of the process and help to "teach" the AI
detection.

During AI detection setup, the input definition step helps to "teach" the
system and enables the AI detection to "learn" by example. Writing a "good" definition
is imperative to the AI detection process.

## Definition guidelines

When adding a definition during AI detection setup, adhere to the following
guidelines. A "good" definition is:

- **Clear and concise:** The definition explains the extraction in a
  straightforward way so that someone without expertise can understand it.
- **General and adaptable:** The definition is not tied to a specific industry
  or use case.
- **Simple language:** The definition does not contain complex legal or
  financial jargon.
- **Exclusion:** The definition provides details about what not to include in
  the AI detection.
- **100–1,000 characters:** The definition is clear without excessive detail
  and does not exceed 1,000 characters.

## Examples of "good" definitions

**NDA**

A Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA) is a legal contract that establishes confidentiality
obligations between parties. It restricts the sharing or use of specified
confidential information and protects sensitive business, financial, or personal
data from unauthorized disclosure.

**Total Contract Value**

Total Contract Value (TCV) refers to the total financial commitment of an agreement. This
includes all fees, charges, and recurring payments over the contract’s full term. It
helps assess the overall value of an agreement for budgeting, financial planning,
and reporting purposes.

## Examples of 'bad' definitions

**NDA**

An NDA is a contract that you sign when joining a startup to make sure you don’t talk
about internal projects. It usually lasts for two years and applies to employees,
contractors, and sometimes investors.

Why is this bad?

- **Too specific:** The definition focuses only on startups and employment
  scenarios.
- **Inaccurate:** NDAs apply to many contexts beyond hiring.
- **Limiting:** The definition does not cover broader use cases like
  partnerships, M&A, or business deals.

**Total Contract Value**

TCV is the total amount a SaaS company expects to make from a single customer’s
three-year subscription, including all renewals and upsells. It helps SaaS finance
teams plan revenue projections.

Why is this bad?

- **Too narrow:** The definition is tied to SaaS businesses and ignores other
  industries.
- **Overly specific:** The definition mentions *three-year subscriptions*
  and *SaaS finance teams*, which limit applicability.
- **Misses the broader purpose:** The definition does not generalize how TCV
  applies across different contract types.
