# Rule Writing Tips for Playbooks in the AI-Assisted Review add-in for Agreement Desk

Read about tips and best practices for writing playbook rules for the Docusign AI-Assisted Review add-in for Agreement Desk.

When writing playbook rules, you are providing instructions for how and when to suggest
changes to your contract. You can use plain language, but you should aim to be
specific. The more specific you are in the rules, the more information you are
giving for recommendations.

It is recommended to write playbook rules as if you were giving instructions to someone
unfamiliar with your business. The add-in will only know what you tell it, so
context helps. When writing rules, you can refine the playbook rule automatically by selecting Refine with AI which will suggest improvements to your rule based on these best practices for more specific, consistent results.

Here are some best practices and examples to follow for writing clear and specific rules.

## 1. Focus on one concept at a time

If there is a rule with multiple instructions, you should separate them into multiple rules. This often results in better suggestions and allows you to build in more details for each rule separately.

Example:

- Instead of: “The contract term should last at least 2 years and governing law is Delaware”, try:
  - Rule 1: The contract term should last at least 2 years.
  - Rule 2: The governing law must be Delaware.

## 2. Be specific about your recommendations

Write your instructions as “if, then” statements about how the contract should be edited. This adds clarity and will provide better instructions. You can also use “greater than”, “less than”, “equal to” for any numerical value.

Examples:

- Instead of “No 3 year terms”, try: “If the term length is 3 years or longer,
  change it to 2 years.”

  Note: In this example, the original rule is ambiguous; it doesn't clarify whether the desired edit is to change an existing 3-year term to a shorter or longer term, or to remove the specified term length entirely. The suggested improvement gives specific instructions about how to edit the contract.
- To remove language entirely, use “delete”, “remove” or similar indications.
  - "Remove any clause about export laws."
  - "Jurisdiction should be Seattle, Washington. If there is some other state listed, delete it entirely."
- You can also use “more,” “less than,” or “equal to” relative to dates and numerical values.
  - “Payments are due no less than 45 days from the invoice receipt
    date”
  - “Termination notices should be more than 10 days”

## 3. Use quotation marks for matching exact text

If you want specific terms mentioned word-for-word in the contract, include the exact
language in your rule.

Examples:

- A data breach or security incident must include “accidental or unlawful loss, alteration, misuse, unauthorized access, or destruction of our data, including all personal data and all data processed by Vendor or its subprocessors”.
- Publicity clause must state “The Company may not use or display Customer’s logo, trademark, trade name, and other mark in or on its website, customer list, or any of its marketing materials.”

## 4. Give context to match specific sections

If your requirement only pertains to a particular clause or section, include section information in the rule. This helps to reduce ambiguity, and ensures an edit targets a particular section of the document.

Examples:

- The definition of confidential information should include product roadmaps.
- The term length information must specify termination for convenience is
  permitted at any time without consent.

  Note: It is not recommended to use section numbers (e.g., “Section 11.4”) to match a given section. A title or description of the section's contents is most helpful here, as outlined in the examples.
